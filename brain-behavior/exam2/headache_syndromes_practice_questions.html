Q1 CONTRAST / DISTINCTION
A medical student is trying to distinguish the three major primary headache types. Which of the following correctly contrasts tension-type headache (TTH) from migraine?
✓ Correct Answer: C — TTH is non-pulsating and mild-to-moderate in severity, whereas migraine is pulsating and moderate-to-severe.
AIncorrect. This is reversed. TTH is typically bilateral (squeezing/pressure), while migraine is classically unilateral — though either can occasionally present on the other side.
BIncorrect. Also reversed. By ICHD-3, TTH allows no more than one of photophobia or phonophobia — never both. Migraine requires both photophobia and phonophobia (or nausea/vomiting). This is one of the highest-yield distinctions in the lecture.
CCorrect. TTH is characterized by a pressing or tightening (non-pulsating) quality at mild-to-moderate intensity — the kind of headache you can push through your day with. Migraine is pulsating and moderate-to-severe, often requiring the patient to stop activity and lie in a dark room.
DIncorrect. Backwards. TTH has NO nausea or vomiting by definition. Migraine is frequently associated with nausea and/or vomiting — a core criterion D feature in ICHD-3.
EIncorrect. These features describe migraine. Migraine lasts 4–72 hours and IS aggravated by routine activity. TTH lasts 30 minutes to 7 days and is notably not aggravated by routine activity.
Q2 CLINICAL VIGNETTE
A 38-year-old male smoker presents with episodic, excruciating left-sided periorbital pain that wakes him from sleep. Each attack lasts about 45 minutes, occurs once or twice daily, and has been happening every autumn for the past three years. During attacks he paces the room and cannot sit still. His left eye shows ptosis and miosis. Between attacks he feels completely normal. What is the most likely diagnosis?
✓ Correct Answer: B — Episodic cluster headache
AIncorrect. Migraine lasts 4–72 hours, not 45 minutes. Migraineurs want to lie still in a dark room, not pace. Migraine also lacks prominent ipsilateral Horner-like features and strict seasonal/circadian periodicity.
BCorrect. Textbook cluster headache: male smoker (risk factors), unilateral periorbital pain in V1 distribution, attacks 15 min–3 hours, circadian (wakes from sleep) and circannual (every autumn) periodicity, restlessness/pacing (opposite of migraine), ipsilateral Horner syndrome (ptosis + miosis), and complete return to baseline between attacks. "Episodic" because pain-free periods exceed 3 months.
CIncorrect. TTH is bilateral, mild-to-moderate, non-pulsating, and never produces ipsilateral autonomic features like Horner syndrome or a seasonal attack pattern.
DIncorrect. Trigeminal neuralgia causes brief electric-shock facial pain lasting seconds, triggered by light touch (eating, talking). It does not cause autonomic features, seasonal periodicity, or attacks lasting 45 minutes.
EIncorrect. IIH presents in obese women 20–45 with daily positional headaches, papilledema, and CN VI palsy — not short episodic attacks with Horner syndrome and seasonal clustering.
Q3 FACT / MECHANISM
A patient with migraine reports that during attacks, even a light touch to her scalp is painful, and any physical movement makes the headache worse. Which of the following best explains this phenomenon?
✓ Correct Answer: C — Central and peripheral sensitization following trigeminal activation, lowering the pain threshold so normally non-painful stimuli become painful (allodynia)
AIncorrect. The sphenopalatine ganglion (SPG) contributes to autonomic features seen in cluster headache. It is not the primary mechanism behind cutaneous allodynia during migraine.
BIncorrect. Cortical spreading depression originates in the occipital cortex, not the motor cortex, and causes transient reversible depolarization — not permanent structural changes. It contributes to aura and triggers trigeminal activation.
CCorrect. Step 3 of migraine pathophysiology is sensitization. After CSD activates the trigeminal nucleus caudalis (Step 2), progressive peripheral and central sensitization lowers the pain threshold. This produces allodynia — stimuli that should not be painful (scalp touch, light, sound) become painful — and explains why physical activity exacerbates the headache.
DIncorrect. The older vascular theory proposed vessel dilation caused migraine pain. Current understanding is that primary neural dysfunction drives migraine — the trigeminal system activates first and vascular changes are secondary, not causative.
EIncorrect. The PAG normally inhibits pain via descending pathways. Increased GABA from PAG would enhance inhibition and reduce pain — the opposite of what produces allodynia. Migraine involves dysregulation of this modulatory system.
Q4 FACT / MECHANISM
Using the SNOOP mnemonic for secondary headache red flags, which of the following patient presentations should raise the LEAST concern for a dangerous secondary cause?
✓ Correct Answer: C — A 28-year-old woman with a known 10-year history of migraine who reports her typical unilateral throbbing headache
AIncorrect (this IS a red flag). New headache after age 50 with jaw claudication and temporal pain maps directly to SNOOP "O — Older age" and strongly suggests Giant Cell Arteritis. Risk of irreversible vision loss makes this a medical emergency.
BIncorrect (this IS a red flag). Maximum intensity within 60 seconds = classic thunderclap headache — SNOOP "O — Onset sudden." This is SAH until proven otherwise and requires immediate non-contrast CT followed by LP if CT is negative.
CCorrect — LEAST concerning. A well-established migraineur experiencing her typical, unchanged headache triggers no SNOOP red flags. The critical question is always whether the headache is new, changed, or progressive. A known pattern is reassuring — the concern arises when something is different.
DIncorrect (this IS a red flag). HIV is an explicit secondary risk factor under SNOOP "S." With fever and neck stiffness, this presentation is highly suspicious for meningitis and requires immediate LP and empirical antibiotics. Immunosuppressed patients may present atypically — maintain high suspicion.
EIncorrect (this IS a red flag). Positional headache — worse upright, better lying flat — is SNOOP "P — Positional" and suggests intracranial hypotension (e.g., CSF leak). Neither pattern is consistent with primary headache.
Q5 CLINICAL VIGNETTE
A 32-year-old obese woman presents with daily headaches worse in the morning, brief visual graying when standing, and a pulsatile whooshing sound in her ears. Fundoscopy shows bilateral disc swelling. MRI brain and MR venogram are normal. LP reveals an opening pressure of 310 mmH₂O with completely normal CSF composition. Which of the following represents the definitive long-term treatment for this condition?
✓ Correct Answer: C — Weight loss, with acetazolamide as a pharmacologic adjunct to reduce CSF production
AIncorrect. High-dose corticosteroids treat Giant Cell Arteritis, not IIH. IIH is not an inflammatory condition — it is a disorder of impaired CSF absorption with elevated ICP and completely normal CSF composition.
BIncorrect. Sumatriptan is a triptan for acute migraine and cluster headache. It has no mechanism to reduce intracranial pressure and is not appropriate for IIH management.
CCorrect. The definitive treatment for IIH is weight loss — reliably reduces intracranial pressure by a mechanism not fully understood. Acetazolamide (Diamox) is the standard pharmacologic adjunct; it is a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor that reduces CSF production. Vision-threatening cases may require optic nerve sheath fenestration or CSF shunting.
DIncorrect. Propranolol is a beta-blocker for migraine prevention. It has no established mechanism for lowering intracranial pressure and is not indicated in IIH.
EIncorrect. A therapeutic LP can transiently lower pressure and relieve symptoms but is not a definitive cure — ICP returns within hours to days. Definitive management requires addressing the underlying cause (obesity) plus ongoing pharmacotherapy.
Q6 NOT / INCORRECT
Which of the following is INCORRECT regarding the ICHD-3 diagnostic criteria for migraine without aura?
✓ Correct Answer: C — The headache must be bilateral; unilateral headache excludes the diagnosis.
AIncorrect (A is true, not the answer). ICHD-3 criterion A requires at least 5 attacks fulfilling the remaining criteria — this threshold distinguishes migraine from isolated headache events.
BIncorrect (B is true, not the answer). Criterion B specifies attacks lasting 4–72 hours when untreated or unsuccessfully treated.
CCorrect — this IS the incorrect statement. Migraine is classically unilateral, but ICHD-3 states it can also be bilateral. Unilateral location is just one of the four criterion C features, and only two of four are required. Bilateral migraine does not exclude the diagnosis.
DIncorrect (D is true, not the answer). Criterion D requires at least one of: nausea/vomiting OR both photophobia and phonophobia. This distinguishes migraine from TTH, which allows at most one sensitivity and no nausea.
EIncorrect (E is true, not the answer). Criterion C requires at least 2 of 4 features: unilateral location, pulsating quality, moderate-to-severe intensity, aggravation by routine activity — reflecting the clinical heterogeneity of migraine.
Q7 CLINICAL VIGNETTE
A neurologist wants to prescribe a triptan for acute migraine relief. Which of the following patients would be the most appropriate candidate?
✓ Correct Answer: C — A 34-year-old woman with episodic migraine, normal cardiac history, and well-controlled blood pressure
AIncorrect. Triptans are serotonin (5-HT1B/1D) agonists that cause vasoconstriction. Recent CABG represents active coronary artery disease — a clear contraindication. Triptan-induced coronary vasospasm could precipitate a serious cardiac event.
BIncorrect. Uncontrolled hypertension is a contraindication to triptans. Their vasoconstrictive mechanism can further elevate blood pressure, increasing the risk of stroke or cardiac ischemia. BP must be controlled first.
CCorrect. A patient with episodic migraine, no cardiac disease, and well-controlled BP is the ideal triptan candidate. Triptans are first-line prescription abortive therapy for moderate-to-severe migraine. Subcutaneous formulations work fastest (~15 min); nasal spray and oral tablets offer alternatives with different onset profiles and side effect patterns.
DIncorrect. A history of coronary stenting indicates established CAD — a contraindication to triptans regardless of current stability. Vasoconstrictive agents in patients with fixed coronary obstruction risk inducing ischemia.
EIncorrect. A recent MI is an absolute contraindication to triptans. Alternative acute treatments — such as IV ketorolac, metoclopramide (Reglan), or valproic acid — are appropriate in these cases.
Q8 COMPLETION / STEM
Up to 80% of migraine patients experience significant neck pain during attacks despite having no cervical pathology. The primary anatomical explanation is that trigeminal pain fibers converge with afferents from which structure at the trigeminal cervical complex?
✓ Correct Answer: B — The upper cervical nerve roots (C2–C3)
AIncorrect. The dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus and the periaqueductal gray mediate nausea during migraine — not neck pain. These are autonomic structures involved in the GI symptoms of migraine.
BCorrect. Dural pain fibers (trigeminal origin) converge with afferents from upper cervical nerve roots C2–C3 at the Trigeminal Cervical Complex (TCC). The brain cannot distinguish the source, interpreting pain as coming from both the head AND the neck — this explains neck pain in up to 80% of migraineurs without any structural cervical pathology. The lecturer explicitly emphasized this point.
CIncorrect. CN XI innervates the trapezius and sternocleidomastoid. While these muscles may be secondarily tense, this is not the primary mechanism for neck pain as a direct neurological consequence of trigeminal activation during migraine.
DIncorrect. The nucleus ambiguus provides motor innervation to the pharynx and larynx via CN IX and X. It plays no significant role in migraine-associated neck pain.
EIncorrect. The MLF coordinates conjugate eye movements between vestibular and oculomotor nuclei. Lesions cause internuclear ophthalmoplegia (common in MS). It has no role in the referred neck pain of migraine.
Q9 CLINICAL VIGNETTE
A 47-year-old woman with hypertension presents to the ED with the "worst headache of her life," which began suddenly during exercise and reached full intensity within seconds. She has mild neck stiffness and nausea. She is alert and oriented. Non-contrast CT head is read as normal by radiology. What is the most appropriate next step?
✓ Correct Answer: B — Perform lumbar puncture to assess for xanthochromia or blood in the CSF
AIncorrect — and dangerous. A normal CT does not rule out SAH. CT sensitivity decreases over the first several hours and may miss 2–5% of bleeds early on. Thunderclap headache is SAH until proven otherwise. Discharging without further workup could be fatal.
BCorrect. When CT is negative but clinical suspicion for subarachnoid hemorrhage remains high, lumbar puncture is mandatory. The LP detects xanthochromia — yellow CSF discoloration from bilirubin, a breakdown product of red blood cells — which can be positive even when CT is negative. A positive LP then prompts CTA or MRA to locate the source, followed by DSA if needed to find and treat the aneurysm.
CIncorrect. GCA presents in patients over 50 with temporal headache, jaw claudication, and elevated ESR/CRP — not a thunderclap headache reaching maximum intensity in seconds during exercise in a 47-year-old.
DIncorrect — and dangerous. Topiramate is a preventive migraine medication, not for acute evaluation. Scheduling an outpatient MRI in weeks is wholly inappropriate for a potential SAH — this is an emergency requiring same-visit workup.
EIncorrect. MRI with gadolinium is excellent for many conditions but has lower sensitivity than CT for acute subarachnoid blood in the first 24–48 hours, and is slower. LP is the standard-of-care next step when CT is negative and clinical suspicion remains high.
Q10 CONTRAST / DISTINCTION
A headache specialist is seeing two patients. Patient A has migraines on 10 days per month for the past 6 months and has not tried any preventive medications. Patient B has migraines on 18 days per month (with at least 8 migrainous days) for the past 5 months and has failed two prior preventive medications. Which statement about botulinum toxin (onabotulinumtoxinA) candidacy is correct?
✓ Correct Answer: C — Only Patient B qualifies; botulinum toxin is FDA-approved only for chronic migraine (at least 15 headache days/month with at least 8 migrainous days, for at least 3 months, after failure of at least 2 preventive therapies).
AIncorrect. Patient A has episodic migraine (10 days/month). Botulinum toxin has not been shown effective for episodic migraine and is not FDA-approved for this indication. Clinical trials failed to show benefit below the chronic threshold.
BIncorrect. Reversed. The FDA indication is specifically for chronic migraine, not episodic. Patient A (10 days/month) does not meet the chronic threshold of 15 or more headache days per month.
CCorrect. Botulinum toxin (onabotulinumtoxinA) is FDA-approved exclusively for chronic migraine: at least 15 headache days/month, with at least 8 having migrainous features, for at least 3 months, after failure of at least 2 first-line preventive therapies. Patient B meets all four criteria. The mechanism likely involves reducing CGRP and other neuropeptide release at presynaptic terminals via SNAP-25 inhibition — though the lecturer noted this has not been definitively proven.
DIncorrect. There is no requirement to fail CGRP monoclonal antibodies before botulinum toxin. The prerequisite is failure of two conventional first-line preventive agents (e.g., beta-blockers, antiepileptics, antidepressants). Botulinum toxin and CGRP mAbs are parallel options for chronic migraine prevention.
EIncorrect. This is the opposite of reality. The 15 days/month threshold is the entry criterion for the chronic migraine indication — it is precisely the reason botulinum toxin is indicated, not a contraindication.
